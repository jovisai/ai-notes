Stop Managing People, Start Managing the System

28 Sep 2025
By Muthu Krishnan · AI agent builder with 16+ years of experience building scalable SaaS applications.

As an engineering manager, your instinct is often to manage people. You track individual performance, mediate disputes, and assign tasks. While necessary, this focus has diminishing returns and doesn’t scale. To reach the next level, shift your perspective: stop managing people and start managing the system they operate in.

Your team’s performance is not just a sum of individual talents; it’s a product of its environment. This “system” includes everything from your code review process and deployment pipeline to your communication norms, meeting structures, and even how you celebrate wins. It’s the invisible architecture of how work gets done.

Micromanaging individuals creates dependencies and stifles growth. Architecting a better system creates leverage, autonomy, and a culture of ownership.

How to Start Managing the System Today:

  1. Identify a Bottleneck: Instead of asking, “Why is Jane behind on her task?” ask, “What systemic friction is making Jane’s task difficult?” Is the CI/CD pipeline slow? Are requirements unclear? Is there a lack of documentation? Fixing the system helps not just Jane, but everyone who encounters that friction.

  2. Upgrade Your Processes: Don’t just run the existing playbook—actively design it.

    • Instead of: Assigning every ticket.
    • Try: Creating a clear, well-documented intake and prioritization process that empowers the team to pull work themselves.

  3. Define and Refine Communication Paths:

    • Instead of: Being the hub for all information.
    • Try: Establishing clear channels for different types of communication (e.g., a #dev-ops Slack channel for alerts, a weekly RFC review for architectural decisions). Make information flow predictable and accessible.

  4. Manage Energy, Not Time: A system that constantly burns people out is a failed system.

    • Instead of: Asking for status updates in every 1-on-1.
    • Try: Asking, “What is draining your energy right now?” and “What part of your work is giving you energy?” Use the answers to find and fix systemic energy sinks (like excessive meetings or flaky tests) and amplify energy sources (like pairing sessions or creative autonomy).

By focusing on the system, you transition from being a taskmaster to an architect. You create an environment where talented people can do their best work with minimal friction. This is how you scale your impact, build a resilient organization, and drive long-term excellence.
