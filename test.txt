A lot of leadership teams start with a goal like:

> "How do we grow revenue by 20%?"

The problem is that this question is often too abstract. Nobody wakes up and performs a "20% growth" task.

What people actually do every day are hundreds or thousands of small units of work:

* Answer customer questions
* Create proposals
* Follow up on leads
* Review contracts
* Build features
* Resolve support tickets
* Generate reports
* Onboard customers

Growth emerges from how effectively these individual activities are performed.

So instead of asking:

> "How do we grow 20%?"

Ask:

> "Where does work happen in this company, and where is effort being wasted, delayed, duplicated, or constrained?"

Then optimize those points.

For example:

| Function    | Unit of Work        | Inefficiency                | Impact                 |
| ----------- | ------------------- | --------------------------- | ---------------------- |
| Sales       | Lead qualification  | SDR spends 20 mins per lead | More leads processed   |
| Support     | Ticket triage       | Manual categorization       | Faster response times  |
| Engineering | Code reviews        | Senior engineers bottleneck | More throughput        |
| Finance     | Invoice processing  | Manual entry                | Faster cash collection |
| HR          | Candidate screening | Resume review overload      | Faster hiring          |

Each improvement may only create a 1-5% gain locally.

But if 20 teams each improve 5%, the organization can experience substantial growth without anyone explicitly designing a "20% growth plan."

This is similar to how companies like Amazon think operationally. They rarely start with "increase company output by X%." They obsess over cycle times, defects, bottlenecks, handoffs, and customer friction. The aggregate effect becomes growth.

An even stronger version of your idea might be:

> Organizational growth is often an emergent property of eliminating friction in thousands of individual units of work. Instead of planning growth from the top down, identify where people spend time, where work gets stuck, and where decisions are delayed. Fix enough of those bottlenecks, and growth becomes the natural outcome.

What's particularly interesting in the AI era is that AI gives us a new lens:

Before AI, companies optimized processes.

With AI, companies can optimize individual units of work.

The question shifts from:

> "How do we automate a department?"

to

> "What are the 500 tasks people perform every day, and which of them can be completed faster, better, or not at all?"

That framing feels much more actionable and measurable than a top-down growth target.
