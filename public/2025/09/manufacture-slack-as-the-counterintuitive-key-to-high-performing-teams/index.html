Manufacture Slack as the Counterintuitive Key to High-Performing Teams

27 Sep 2025

As an engineering manager, your instinct is to maximize output by maximizing utilization. Every hour should be accounted for, every engineer fully booked, every sprint packed to capacity. But this relentless focus on 100% efficiency is a trap. The single most powerful, high-leverage skill you can develop is the ability to manufacture slack.

Slack isn’t about encouraging laziness; it’s about building a resilient, innovative, and truly high-performing engineering system. It is the intentional, unallocated capacity that allows your team to breathe, think, and operate beyond the immediate demands of the backlog. A team running at 100% capacity has no buffer for the unexpected and no space for improvement.

Why Slack is a Performance Multiplier, Not a Cost

  1. It Fuels Innovation: The next great idea for your product or a 10x improvement in your CI/CD pipeline won’t come from a ticket. It will come from an engineer who had the free time to experiment, read documentation for a new tool, or prototype a solution to a problem they’ve been mulling over. Slack is the fertile ground where innovation grows.

  2. It Builds Resilience: Production issues, urgent security patches, and unexpected stakeholder requests are inevitable. In a 100% utilized team, these events cause chaos, derail sprints, and lead to burnout. A team with slack can absorb these shocks, addressing emergencies calmly and effectively without sacrificing long-term goals.

  3. It Eliminates Hidden Waste: When teams are rushed, they take shortcuts. They accrue tech debt, skip writing documentation, and avoid necessary refactoring. This creates drag that slows down all future development. Slack gives engineers the time to do things right, sharpen their tools, and pay down the debt that grinds velocity to a halt.

How to Start Manufacturing Slack Immediately

  • Plan for 80% Capacity: When planning sprints or cycles, aim to load the team to no more than 80% of their historical velocity. The remaining 20% is your strategic buffer. It’s not “empty” time; it’s the capacity that will be used for all the essential work that isn’t on the backlog: mentoring, fixing flaky tests, and handling the unexpected.

  • Timebox, Don’t Ticket, Exploration: Formalize slack with structures like “Innovation Days,” “Fix-it Fridays,” or a dedicated afternoon each week for self-directed work. The only rule is that it must be used to make the team, the product, or the codebase better.

  • Become a Shield: Your primary role is to protect the team from the “tyranny of the urgent.” Learn to differentiate between true emergencies and manufactured urgency. Push back on requests that can wait, and fiercely guard your team’s capacity to focus on planned work and maintain their slack.

By shifting your mindset from a resource optimizer to a system builder, you’ll discover that the key to going faster isn’t to push the accelerator to the floor—it’s to build a system that has the space to adapt, innovate, and endure. Manufacture slack, and watch your team’s performance and health soar.
