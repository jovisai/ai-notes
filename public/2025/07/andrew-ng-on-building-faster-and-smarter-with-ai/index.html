Andrew Ng on Building Faster and Smarter with AI

11 Jul 2025

Here are the key takeaways from the video by Andrew Ng on Building faster and Smarter with AI

  • Speed is a Strong Predictor of Startup Success: Execution speed is highly correlated with a startup’s odds of success, and new AI technology is enabling startups to move much faster. Best practices for achieving this speed are rapidly evolving, changing every two to three months.

  • Biggest Opportunities are in the Application Layer:

    • While much of the excitement and hype surrounds semiconductor, cloud, and foundation model companies, the largest opportunities are “almost by definition” at the application layer.
    • Applications are essential to generate the revenue needed to support the underlying technology layers.

  • Agentic AI is a Game-Changer:

    • The rise of agentic AI is considered the most important tech trend in AI.
    • Unlike linear prompting, agentic workflows allow AI systems to perform iterative processes like outlining, research, drafting, critiquing, and revising, leading to a much better work product, even if slower in individual steps.
    • These workflows are crucial for complex tasks such as processing compliance or legal documents and medical diagnoses.
    • A new “agentic orchestration layer” has emerged, making it easier for application builders to coordinate calls to various underlying technology layers.

  • Focus on Concrete Ideas for Speed:

    • A concrete product idea is specified in enough detail for an engineer to build it quickly.
    • Vague ideas, though they may receive “kudos,” prevent rapid development and validation. Concrete ideas allow for quick testing and discovery of whether they work or not.

  • Rapid Decision-Making is Essential:

    • Subject matter experts with a “good gut” feeling can often make faster and surprisingly good decisions than relying on slow data collection.
    • Startups should pursue one clear hypothesis at a time, pivoting quickly if data indicates the idea isn’t working.
    • Constantly changing direction based on every new data point might signal an insufficient knowledge base in the sector.

  • AI Coding Assistance Revolutionizes Engineering Speed:

    • AI coding assistance significantly increases engineering speed and reduces costs.
    • It makes building “quick and dirty prototypes” easily 10 times faster, or even more, due to reduced requirements for integration, reliability, scalability, and security.
    • This enables startups to systematically build many prototypes (e.g., 20) to quickly test what works, as the cost of proof-of-concepts has plummeted.
    • The approach should be “move fast and be responsible,” rather than just “move fast and break things”.
    • The cost of software engineering has decreased so much that code itself is now a less valuable artifact, making it feasible to completely rebuild codebases multiple times or change tech stacks that were previously considered “one-way doors”.

  • Empower Everyone to Code:

    • The advice not to learn to code due to AI automation is “some of the worst career advice ever given”.
    • As coding becomes easier with AI tools, more people, including those in non-engineering roles (e.g., CFO, HR, recruiters), should learn to code to enhance their productivity.
    • The ability to precisely tell a computer what you want, whether through prompting or steering AI, is a crucial skill for the future.

  • Product Management Becomes the New Bottleneck:

    • As engineering has become significantly faster, the work of product management—getting user feedback and deciding what features to build—is increasingly becoming the bottleneck.
    • Traditional product manager-to-engineer ratios (e.g., 1 PM to 4-7 engineers) are shifting, with proposals even suggesting more PMs than engineers.
    • Entrepreneurs need a portfolio of tactics for rapid product feedback, ranging from relying on their own gut (if an expert) to asking friends, strangers in public places, sending prototypes to testers, and finally, AB testing, which is now considered one of the slowest tactics.
    • The goal of feedback is not just to make immediate decisions but to hone one’s instincts and update mental models for future, faster, high-quality decisions.

  • Understanding AI Technology Provides a Competitive Edge:

    • Teams that genuinely understand AI have a significant advantage because expertise in implementing AI effectively is not yet widespread.
    • Correct technical decisions can resolve problems in days, while incorrect ones can lead to months of wasted effort.
    • Knowing the various AI “building blocks” (e.g., prompting, evals, guardrails, RAG, embeddings, fine-tuning) allows for combining them in exponentially richer ways to build novel applications.

  • Responsible AI Application is Key:

    • AI is a powerful tool with potential for both good and harm, but its safety is a function of how it is applied, not an inherent property of the technology itself. Instead of “AI safety,” it’s more appropriate to think about “responsible AI”.
    • It is crucial to build products that genuinely make people better off; AI Fund has even killed projects with solid economic cases on ethical grounds.

  • Beware of Overhyped Narratives:

    • Many “hype narratives” around AI (e.g., human extinction, widespread job loss, massive compute needs, casual destruction of startups by new models) are often “ridiculous” or “not true” and are amplified for promotional, fundraising, or influence purposes.
    • These narratives can be weaponized against open-source software, as some businesses seek to become gatekeepers of large-scale foundation models through burdensome regulations.
    • Protecting open-source/open-weight models is a crucial ongoing fight to prevent the stifling of innovation and ensure the diffusion of AI knowledge.

  • Focus on Product-Market Fit First:

    • When starting a business, the number one priority is to build a product that users truly love.
    • Moats are often overhyped; businesses typically evolve into defensible positions after achieving product-market fit.
    • There’s currently a vast “white space” of opportunities in the application layer, exceeding the number of skilled people available to build them.

  • Education with AI is Evolving:

    • AI in education is still in an experimental phase, with potential for both making teachers more productive and enabling hyper-personalized learning through personal tutors. The exact end state of this transformation is not yet clear.
    • The vision of AI making everything “so easy” was hype; complex workflows in education still require mapping to agentic workflows.
