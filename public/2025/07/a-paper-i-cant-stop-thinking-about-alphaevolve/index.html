A Paper I Can't Stop Thinking About: AlphaEvolve

06 Jul 2025

Every so often, you read a paper that just sticks with you. Not because of one specific result, but because of the shift in perspective it represents. For me, that paper is DeepMind’s recent work on AlphaEvolve.

I’ve been trying to articulate what makes it feel so different. It’s not just another coding assistant or a clever new model architecture. There’s a sense of genuine, autonomous discovery here that feels like a new chapter beginning. It’s one of those things that makes you sit back and think, “Okay, this is where it gets interesting.”

At its core, the idea is surprisingly elegant. The team took the old-school concept of evolutionary algorithms and fused it with the creative, pattern-matching power of modern large language models.

It works sort of like you’d imagine. You start with a population of clumsy, barely-functional programs. You test them, find the ones that are slightly less terrible than the others, and keep them. Then—and this is the key—you use an LLM to intelligently “mutate” the code. It doesn’t just randomly flip bits; it suggests meaningful, creative changes. This new generation of programs is then tested, and the cycle repeats.

It’s a simple feedback loop, but it’s powerful. Evolution provides the relentless, grinding pressure to improve, while the LLM provides the spark of insight.

flowchart TB
    A[Start with a bunch of random programs] --> B[Test how well they work]
    B --> C[Keep the best ones]
    C --> D[Mix and match the code]
    D --> E[Use an LLM to suggest smart improvements]
    E --> F[Test the new programs]
    F --> C
    C --> G[Get a final, super-efficient program]

Honestly, this would just be a cool idea if it stayed in the lab. But the results are what really got me.

This isn’t a toy system solving toy problems. AlphaEvolve has outperformed human-tuned heuristics in Google’s own data centers. It has discovered more compact representations for logic circuits. Most impressively, it independently rediscovered a famous multiplication algorithm (Karatsuba’s algorithm) and then went on to find new ones that are even faster for specific use cases.

That last part is what I can’t get out of my head. It didn’t just find a solution we already knew; it found something better. It’s a subtle but profound difference. We’re moving from AI that can find answers in its training data to AI that can generate genuinely new knowledge.

It feels like we’re on the cusp of a transition from “code completion” to “code discovery.” This isn’t about making developers faster; it’s about creating a new kind of scientific tool, or maybe even a new kind of scientist. An autonomous research assistant that can explore, experiment, and learn on its own.

Of course, it’s easy to get carried away. The researchers are very clear about the limitations. This process is incredibly compute-intensive, and its success still hinges on humans setting up the right problems and reward functions. And we’re still a long way from being able to formally verify the correctness of the code it produces, which is a major hurdle for using it in critical systems. So no, this isn’t AGI.

But it feels like a meaningful step. It’s more autonomous than a chatbot, more creative than the symbolic systems that came before it.

Reading this paper, I don’t feel a sense of dread or a fear of being replaced. I feel a sense of excitement. It hints at a future where our tools are not just passive instruments but active collaborators in discovery. A future where a startup could evolve its own infrastructure code, or a scientist could partner with an AI to co-design new molecules.

AlphaEvolve is one of those projects that feels like a blueprint for what’s next. It’s a beautiful synthesis of ideas—LLM creativity, evolutionary rigor, and scientific validation. If you’re in this field, it’s one to watch. It’s one I’ll be thinking about for a long time.
