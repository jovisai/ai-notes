# The Doorman Fallacy and Why AI Keeps Failing at Replacing People

In 2023, AI was supposed to replace programmers.

In 2024, AI was supposed to replace customer support.

In 2025, executives were talking about replacing entire departments.

Today, many of those same companies are quietly re-hiring people they let go.

Not because AI is useless.

Because they made a mistake that is common, predictable, and expensive.

It even has a name.

**The Doorman Fallacy.**

## The Assumption Behind AI Replacement

For the last few years, we've been told that AI is becoming smarter, cheaper, and more capable.

Every benchmark seemed to confirm the trend.

* Higher scores
* More tokens processed
* More code generated
* More tasks completed

The conclusion seemed obvious.

> If AI can perform the visible part of a job, eventually it can perform the entire job.

That sounds reasonable.

Until companies actually try it.

That's where the story starts to break down.

## The Doorman Fallacy

Advertising expert and behavioral economist Rory Sutherland describes a concept called the **Doorman Fallacy**.

Imagine a luxury hotel.

Management notices they are paying someone $30,000 a year to stand at the entrance and open doors.

From a spreadsheet perspective, the solution seems obvious.

Install automatic doors.

Save the money.

Problem solved.

Except six months later:

* Customer satisfaction drops
* Repeat bookings decline
* Revenue starts slipping

What happened?

The doorman was never just opening doors.

He was:

* Welcoming guests
* Recognizing regular customers
* Providing security
* Helping with luggage
* Creating a premium experience

The visible task was opening doors.

The real value was everything surrounding that task.

## How Companies Apply the Same Logic to AI

Many organizations evaluate jobs the same way.

They reduce a role to its most obvious output.

* Customer service becomes answering questions
* Programming becomes writing code
* Management becomes sending emails
* Design becomes creating graphics

Then they ask:

> If AI can do that part, why do we still need people?

That's the fallacy.

Because the visible output is often the least valuable part of the role.

## Software Development Is a Perfect Example

Many non-technical leaders assume developers are paid to write code.

Experienced engineers know that's rarely true.

Writing code is often the easiest part of the job.

The difficult parts are:

* Understanding ambiguous requirements
* Evaluating trade-offs
* Designing architecture
* Debugging unusual edge cases
* Predicting future failures
* Maintaining systems for years
* Making decisions under uncertainty

In other words, the hard part is thinking.

The code is simply the artifact produced by that thinking.

## Where AI Excels and Where It Struggles

Modern language models can generate code at incredible speed.

But generating code is not the same as understanding a system.

It is not the same as:

* Owning decisions
* Understanding business context
* Predicting long-term consequences
* Balancing competing constraints
* Knowing which shortcuts become future disasters

This explains a pattern many teams are seeing.

The more experienced the developer, the less impressed they tend to be by raw code generation alone.

Not because the technology is bad.

Because they understand how much of software engineering happens outside the editor.

The code is the final output.

The real work happened before the first line was written.

## The Same Problem Appears Everywhere

Software is not unique.

Many industries are running into the same issue.

Companies that replaced customer support teams with AI discovered customers often disliked the experience.

Fast-food chains deployed AI ordering systems and encountered highly public failures.

Organizations that aggressively reduced headcount are now bringing employees back.

In most cases, AI did not completely fail.

The failure was in how the job was defined.

Organizations optimized for the visible task and ignored everything surrounding it.

They removed the doorman.

Then discovered the doorman was doing much more than opening doors.

## The Question Most People Are Asking Wrong

When someone says:

"AI writes code now."

Or:

"AI answers customer questions now."

They're often measuring the wrong thing.

The real question isn't:

> Can AI perform the task?

The real question is:

> Can AI replace all of the invisible value that surrounds the task?

That invisible value is where judgment, context, accountability, trust, relationships, and experience live.

And so far, that is where reality keeps colliding with the hype.

The biggest mistake companies make is assuming a job is the output they can see.

The output is usually the easy part.

The value is everything they can't see.
